Abstract

Reclaiming Focus and Happiness in an Age of Distraction

In an era saturated by digital technology, the constant interruptions of social media keep people in a chronic state of distraction, while our own restless streams of thought continually pull attention away from the present moment. This pervasive distraction not only undermines cognitive functioning but also erodes well-being. How to cultivate present-moment focus and happiness has therefore become a central question for researchers and practitioners alike.

Taking present-moment focus and well-being as its core constructs, this dissertation examines them across three levels: psychological mechanisms, personal practice, and interpersonal influence. Chapter One works at the level of mechanism, asking how external motivation weakens the positive relationship between focus and happiness, and examining the buffering role that trait self-compassion plays when the mind wanders. Chapter Two turns to personal practice, testing how nature-based and mindfulness-based interventions raise everyday focus and well-being. Chapter Three extends the lens to interpersonal influence, exploring how more mindful individuals improve the quality of interpersonal interaction, and how a chatbot cast in a mindful role shapes the expression of interpersonal gratitude.

Together, these studies identify the factors that shape focus and happiness at a theoretical level, and offer concrete implications for cultivating well-being — our own and other people's — in practice.

Chapter One

Psychological Mechanisms

How do focus and distraction shape happiness?

Using experience sampling to capture momentary states of focus in daily life, this chapter asks how external motivation weakens the positive relationship from focus to happiness, and how trait self-compassion buffers the cost of mind-wandering.

Study 1How motivation shapes the path from focus to happiness Published · Mindfulness (2025) ▾

Research questionFocusing on the present moment makes people happier — so why is the effect sometimes so faint? This study tests whether the motivation behind an activity moderates the relationship between focus and happiness, and whether people high in trait mindfulness are more sensitive to that motivation.

MethodFifty-six Taiwanese university students were followed for two weeks with experience sampling, reporting three times a day at random on how focused they were, how happy they felt, and the type of motivation behind the activity at hand (autonomous or external).

Key findingsPresent-moment focus significantly and positively predicted happiness; the more externally motivated the activity, however, the weaker that positive relationship became. People high in trait mindfulness were more attuned to their own motivation, which amplified rather than reduced the undermining effect of external motivation — suggesting that mindfulness training should also work to lower the influence of external motivation.

Study 2When the mind wanders, the motivation behind the thought shapes happiness Dissertation chapter ▾

Research questionDoes mind-wandering always make people unhappy? This study is the first to approach the question through the motivational content of the wandering thought: does drifting toward something autonomously motivated (a gathering you look forward to) differ from drifting toward something controlled (work that simply has to be done)? And what roles do age and self-compassion play?

MethodNinety-one participants (35 older adults, 56 younger adults) were followed for two weeks by experience sampling, yielding 3,023 momentary reports of daily experience. Trait self-compassion was also measured, and the data analyzed with multilevel moderated mediation models.

Key findingsParticipants were mind-wandering 39.3% of the time on average, and were less happy when they were. Yet wandering toward autonomously motivated content was significantly happier than wandering toward controlled content — during controlled activities, mind-wandering toward autonomous content left happiness on par with being focused, suggesting that mind-wandering can compensate for a lack of autonomy. Older adults, moreover, showed no drop in happiness when their minds wandered, because their higher trait self-compassion (with mindfulness and common humanity as the key components) buffered the cost.

Chapter One Summary · The Fragility and the Safeguards of Focus and Happiness▾

Fragility: The positive link between focus and happiness is not robust — external motivation can break it. At the organizational level, building environments that support autonomy reduces that interference.

Safeguards: Self-compassion softens the negative effect of mind-wandering. Younger adults can learn from older ones: notice the moment attention drifts, meet it with self-compassion, and rebuild the resilience that keeps distraction from taking a toll.

Chapter Two

Personal Practice

Which interventions actually strengthen focus and happiness?

Drawing on a two-track approach — nudge theory (changing the environment) and empowerment theory (systematic training) — this chapter takes a longitudinal view of how nature-based and mindfulness-based interventions raise everyday focus and well-being.

Study 3Nature-based intervention, focus, and happiness Published · J. Environ. Psychol. (2026) ▾

Research questionContact with nature improves well-being, but through what psychological mechanism? This study proposes that mindfulness state is the key mediator: in nature, it is easier to return to the present moment.

MethodTwo sub-studies: the first used experience sampling to compare happiness, stress, and autonomous motivation during the intervention with baseline days; the second used the day reconstruction method to measure happiness and connectedness to nature. Both were analyzed with multilevel regression.

Key findingsRelative to baseline days, the nature-based intervention raised happiness, lowered stress, and increased both autonomous motivation and connectedness to nature. More importantly, mindfulness state significantly mediated the relationship between the intervention and well-being, and moments of higher mindfulness during the intervention were also moments of greater happiness — future nature-based programs may strengthen their effects by building in mindfulness elements.

Study 4Mindfulness intervention, focus, and happiness Dissertation chapter ▾

Research questionThe benefit of a mindfulness course depends on how much people actually practice. This study proposes an asymmetric U-shaped model of mindfulness practice: below a certain threshold, mindfulness may lower rather than raise well-being; only above it does a dose-response effect appear.

MethodTwenty-one participants completed an eight-week Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) course, alongside 27 age- and gender-matched controls (a reading task). All kept a daily well-being diary for 50 consecutive days (personal, interpersonal, environmental, and physical dimensions) and completed three computer-based tasks — sustained attention, emotional memory, and eye tracking of emotional faces — before and after the course.

Key findingsUsing an average of 10 minutes of daily practice as the cut point, the mindfulness group split into high practicers (about 25 min/day) and low practicers (about 5 min/day). High practicers showed significantly higher trait mindfulness and higher daily well-being on every dimension than controls; below roughly 5 minutes a day, well-being dipped slightly instead, supporting the asymmetric U-shaped curve. Mindfulness training mainly reduced internally generated distraction rather than improving objective attention, supporting an internal-attention account; low practicers showed a decline in metamemory for positive high-arousal stimuli, echoing the beginner's dilemma. Cross-lagged analyses further showed that practice, well-being, and sleep quality nourish one another in both directions over time.

Chapter Two Summary · Pathways That Strengthen Focus and Happiness▾

Deep connection, not passive exposure: simply being in a natural setting is not what matters. Well-being comes from engaging with it attentively and without judgment — looking at the landscape mindfully, savoring food mindfully, building a deep connection with the natural world.

Sustained practice, not just finishing the course: taking a mindfulness course without practicing may actually lower mindfulness state. What raises well-being is daily accumulation — keeping at least five minutes of self-directed practice a day, and letting the positive cycle between well-being and sleep quality feed the motivation to keep going.

Chapter Three

Interpersonal Influence

How do focus and mindfulness shape other people and our relationships?

Extending the lens from the individual to the interpersonal: how people higher in trait mindfulness improve the quality of intergenerational communication, and how a generative AI chatbot cast in a mindful role reshapes the expression of interpersonal gratitude.

Study 5Trait mindfulness and the quality of communication Published · Current Psychology (2025) ▾

Research questionHow do intrapersonal mindfulness (present-moment focus, non-judgment) and interpersonal mindfulness (attending to verbal and nonverbal cues in conversation) each shape the quality of intergenerational communication?

MethodForty-four Taiwanese intergenerational dyads (a younger and an older adult) completed three communication scenarios. Communication quality was assessed on emotional, cognitive, and behavioral dimensions following the interpersonal chemistry model, and analyzed with multilevel regression and the Actor–Partner Interdependence Model (APIM).

Key findingsBoth forms of mindfulness increased positive affect, lowered stress, strengthened empathy and communication satisfaction, narrowed psychological distance, and produced greater consensus on contentious topics; interpersonal mindfulness carried the broader benefit (it also reduced negative affect and sped up cooperation). APIM analyses showed that mindfulness improved communication quality more comprehensively for older adults than for younger ones. Intriguingly, body-synchrony analyses revealed that mindfulness promoted anti-phase synchrony — when one person speaks (and moves), the other listens attentively (and stays still) — the embodied signature of attentive listening.

Study 6A mindful chatbot and interpersonal gratitude Published · CHB: Artificial Humans (2026) ▾

Research questionWhen generative AI enters a gratitude practice, it changes not only the outcome but the process. This study compares AI-based conversational gratitude with a traditional gratitude journal to examine how generative AI reshapes well-being interventions.

MethodA seven-day randomized controlled trial with three arms: a gratitude chatbot group (conversational writing with generative AI), a gratitude journal group (structured writing prompts), and an active control group (recording daily events), analyzed with linear mixed-effects models combined with thematic analysis (mixed methods).

Key findingsBoth forms of gratitude raised happiness and meaning relative to control; the chatbot group, however, reported lower perceived effort, expressed gratitude more shallowly (more recognizing, less thanking), and leaned toward self-oriented gratitude (having, losing, comparing). Qualitative analysis showed participants were surprised and inspired by the AI-generated summaries. Overall, generative AI cuts both ways: it lowers the cognitive barrier and sparks insight, yet may erode reflective depth — future AI well-being tools should let users decide for themselves whether to bring AI into the loop.

Chapter Three Summary · How Focus and Happiness Spill Over▾

Education: bringing mindfulness training into schools raises trait mindfulness in students, parents, and teachers alike, improving the quality of intergenerational communication.

Technology design: AI can be designed around the core elements of mindfulness and deployed in contexts of interpersonal gratitude, so that its development becomes a help to human relationships rather than an obstacle.

Takeaways

In an Age of Scarce Attention, Focus Is Worth Protecting

Psychological Level

Strengthen autonomous motivation and cultivate self-compassion — an inner safeguard for focus.

Practical Level

Actively build a deep connection with nature and with mindfulness — a mechanism that strengthens focus.

Societal Level

Let the value of mindfulness guide education and technology design, so that living in the present becomes a collective choice of our time.

In the age of AI, the scarcest resource is not computing power but human attention.
To hold on to the present is to hold on to what makes us human.
